Spike Detection · Real Public Data

TL;DR — Built a spike detector on real Wikipedia pageview data: 103 articles, 92 days, July through September 2024. To grade it honestly, I planted four fake spikes with known sizes and dates (an answer key), kept at realistic 2× to 5× lifts, and the detector caught all four, including a ×2 slow burn and a one-day blip that a persistence-only rule would miss. Bonus: the same detector surfaced the real news of that summer for free, the Paris Olympics, the July 13 rally shooting, Taylor Swift’s Harris endorsement, and Obama’s DNC speech all show up as ranked events.

Data source: Daily per-article pageview counts from the official Wikimedia Pageviews REST API (public domain, CC0). The fetch script (data/fetch_wikipedia.py) pulls July 1 to September 30, 2024 for a curated set of 103 well-known en.wikipedia articles and caches it as month-partitioned Parquet. The real data is untouched except for four labeled spike events I plant on top so the validation step has ground truth. Which four, and why, is spelled out below.

The Business Problem

In media measurement you face the same question constantly: did a traffic spike actually mean something, or is it noise? A 30% jump in sessions might be a successful TV spot, a viral social moment, or just a normal Saturday.

Answering that manually doesn’t scale. With daily data across many pages, products, or regions, you need an automated framework that:

  • Builds a dynamic baseline per series instead of one global threshold
  • Flags spikes that are statistically unusual, not just big in absolute terms
  • Handles both flavors of spike: the one-day blip and the multi-day slow burn
  • Ranks what it finds, because an alert list nobody can triage is the same as no alert list

Wikipedia pageviews are a perfect testbed. The data is real, it’s public, and the summer of 2024 (Olympics, an assassination attempt, a presidential race) is packed with genuine media-driven spikes to find.

The Approach

1

Ingest real data to Parquet

Pull daily pageviews from the Wikimedia REST API for 103 articles over 92 days, write to month-partitioned Parquet, query lazily with Polars.

2

Plant a known answer key

Inject four labeled spikes with chosen multipliers and durations (×2 to ×5, 1 to 4 days) so detection quality can be measured, not eyeballed.

3

Build robust baselines

Rolling 21-day median plus MAD-scaled sigma per article. Median and MAD don’t get corrupted by the very outliers we’re hunting.

4

Detect in two tiers, then rank

A persistent tier (z > 5 for 2+ consecutive days) and an extreme tier (z > 12 for even a single day). Group flagged days into events and rank by peak z for triage.

Step 1: Real data, real ingestion

Wikipedia publishes per-article pageview counts through the Pageviews REST API. The per-article endpoint is daily grain (the hourly variant was retired; hourly still exists as multi-GB bulk dumps at ~7M rows per hour, which is where this exact pipeline would point in production). Each row is one (article, day) pair: article, timestamp, views.

The fetch script already cached everything as partitioned Parquet, so loading is a lazy Polars scan. I time it because the query speed is part of the point.

Step 2: The partitioned Parquet warehouse

The engineering payoff is in the storage layer. The fetch script writes Hive-style month-partitioned Parquet (year_month=YYYY-MM/), the same pattern the Data Foundation page describes, just at a coarser grain than production hourly data would use.

Note

On partition grain. I deliberately partition by month, not by day, even though the data is daily. With 103 articles × 92 days, a per-day partition would mean ~100 rows per file, small enough that Parquet’s per-file metadata overhead dominates the actual column data and the format loses to plain CSV. Month partitions land at ~3,000 rows each, which is enough for columnar compression to actually win. The same dataset at hourly bulk-dump grain (~7M rows/hour) would partition by date with ease. Pick the partition column to match the grain of the data, not the calendar.

Step 3: Plant the answer key

Here’s the part most anomaly detection writeups skip. If I only run the detector on raw data and point at dramatic charts, you have to take my word that the flags are good. Real spikes don’t arrive labeled. So before detection, I plant four fake spikes with exact known dates, sizes, and durations on top of the real data. That gives the validation step an answer key: we know precisely what a perfect detector should find, because we put it there.

The four are chosen to stress different failure modes, not picked at random:

Article Planted scenario Multiplier Duration What it tests
Albert_Einstein “Breaking News” ×4 2 days starting Jul 21 a sharp burst on a steady page
Taylor_Swift “Celebrity Mention” ×3 3 days starting Aug 5 a spike on a page that is already busy and noisy
Bitcoin “Viral Social” ×2 4 days starting Aug 25 the smallest lift but the longest, a slow burn
Elon_Musk “TV Mention” ×5 1 day on Sep 9 a one-day blip, the case a persistence rule misses

The scenario names are just labels I made up for the shape of each event, they aren’t real news stories. ×N means every real pageview count inside that window gets multiplied by N, so the spike sits on top of the page’s genuine day-to-day texture instead of being a clean synthetic curve.

The sizes are deliberately modest. In my experience real media-driven lifts land somewhere between 2× and 5× of normal activity, a true 8× or 10× day is rare. An earlier draft of this study planted ×8 and ×12 spikes and they were almost insulting to the detector, anything would catch those. The ×2 Bitcoin slow burn is the one that actually makes the detector work for it.

Step 4: The detection algorithm

Robust statistics matter here. Mean and standard deviation break down when outliers are the thing you’re trying to detect. The outlier inflates the standard deviation, which raises the threshold, which masks the next outlier. A classic feedback loop (there’s a chart of this failure further down).

The fix is median + MAD (median absolute deviation). The median ignores extreme values, and MAD scaled by 1.4826 is a consistent estimator of the standard deviation under normality, so the z-scores stay interpretable.

Detection runs in two tiers, because real spikes come in two shapes:

  • Persistent tier: robust z > 5 for at least 2 consecutive days. Catches sustained events while filtering one-day noise. This is the tier that earns the ×2 Bitcoin slow burn.
  • Extreme tier: robust z > 12 on any single day. Exists because a genuinely large one-day event (our planted ×5 Elon Musk day lands around z ≈ 47) should not be thrown away just because it didn’t persist.
Robust tiered spike detection: rolling median + MAD 
### tuned on this dataset: z>5 alone flagged too much (daily wiki traffic is
### heavy tailed), z>5 with 2-day persistence missed the 1-day planted spike.
### two tiers handle both shapes.
WINDOW_DAYS = 21      ## 3 weeks of trailing history for the baseline
Z_PERSISTENT = 5.0    ## threshold for the persistent tier
Z_EXTREME = 12.0      ## a single day this far out counts on its own
MIN_PERSIST = 2       ## consecutive days required in the persistent tier

def detect_spikes_for_article(grp):
    """robust two-tier spike detection for one article's daily series"""
    grp = grp.sort_values('timestamp').reset_index(drop=True)

    ## rolling robust baseline
    grp['rolling_median'] = grp['views'].rolling(WINDOW_DAYS, min_periods=7).median()
    grp['rolling_mad'] = (
        grp['views'].rolling(WINDOW_DAYS, min_periods=7)
        .apply(lambda x: np.median(np.abs(x - np.median(x))), raw=True)
    )
    ### scale MAD so it's comparable to a std dev under normality
    grp['rolling_sigma'] = grp['rolling_mad'] * 1.4826

    ## z-score against the robust baseline
    grp['robust_z'] = (
        (grp['views'] - grp['rolling_median'])
        / grp['rolling_sigma'].replace(0, np.nan)
    )

    ## tier 1: persistent. find runs of consecutive days above Z_PERSISTENT
    ## and keep whole runs that last at least MIN_PERSIST days
    grp['above'] = grp['robust_z'] > Z_PERSISTENT
    run_id = (~grp['above']).cumsum()
    run_len = grp.groupby(run_id)['above'].transform('sum')
    grp['persistent'] = grp['above'] & (run_len >= MIN_PERSIST)

    ## tier 2: extreme. one day this far above baseline stands on its own
    grp['extreme'] = grp['robust_z'] > Z_EXTREME

    grp['is_spike'] = grp['persistent'] | grp['extreme']
    return grp

## manual loop instead of groupby().apply(), reads cleaner and dodges the
## include_groups deprecation dance in pandas 2.2+
per_article = []
for article_name, grp in df.groupby('article'):
    res = detect_spikes_for_article(grp.copy())
    res['article'] = article_name
    per_article.append(res)
results = pd.concat(per_article, ignore_index=True)

## group consecutive flagged days into distinct events, then rank.
## an alert list is only useful if it's ranked
flagged = results[results['is_spike']].copy()
flagged['gap'] = flagged.groupby('article')['timestamp'].diff().dt.days.fillna(99)
flagged['event_id'] = (flagged['gap'] > 1).groupby(flagged['article']).cumsum()

events = (
    flagged.groupby(['article', 'event_id'])
    .agg(
        start=('timestamp', 'min'),
        n_days=('is_spike', 'sum'),
        peak_z=('robust_z', 'max'),
        peak_views=('views', 'max'),
        baseline=('rolling_median', 'median'),
    )
    .reset_index()
    .sort_values('peak_z', ascending=False)
    .reset_index(drop=True)
)
events['lift'] = (events['peak_views'] / events['baseline']).round(1)

## peak date per event, the charts use this
peak_dates = []
for _, r in events.iterrows():
    sub = flagged[(flagged['article'] == r['article']) & (flagged['event_id'] == r['event_id'])]
    peak_dates.append(sub.loc[sub['robust_z'].idxmax(), 'timestamp'])
events['peak_date'] = peak_dates

n_events = len(events)
n_flagged_articles = events['article'].nunique()
print(f"{n_events} distinct events across {n_flagged_articles} of {n_articles} articles")

show = events.head(12).copy()

Step 5: Validate against the answer key

Now the graded part. For each planted event, did the detector flag the actual planted days, and which tier caught it?

Why median + MAD instead of mean + std

What this demonstrates

The methodology: spike detection with median + MAD instead of mean + std, because the anomalies you’re hunting will corrupt classical statistics (the comparison chart above shows exactly how). Two detection tiers because one-day blips and slow burns are both real and one rule can’t serve both.

The validation: I planted four spikes with known sizes and dates and graded the detector against them, instead of pointing at charts and declaring victory. The grading is day-level, not “did the article show up somewhere in the flag list.”

The honesty: the thresholds were tuned on this dataset and I say so. The planted events measure recall, not precision, and I say that too. The real events the detector surfaced (Olympics, July 13, the DNC) are the strongest evidence it works, precisely because I didn’t plant them.

What I’d extend in a production version

  • CUSUM or Bayesian online changepoint detection for slow level shifts, the MAD approach is built for sharp spikes
  • Per-series threshold learning — one global z threshold across 103 very different pages is the biggest simplification here; volatility profiles differ by page
  • Cause attribution — join detected events against news APIs, social trends, or a media schedule so events arrive pre-explained instead of pre-ranked
  • Streaming implementation — rolling median and MAD both have incremental versions, so this ports to a Kafka consumer for sub-minute detection at the hourly bulk-dump grain

## The Business Problem

In media measurement you face the same question constantly: **did a traffic spike actually
mean something, or is it noise?** A 30% jump in sessions might be a successful TV spot, a
viral social moment, or just a normal Saturday.

Answering that manually doesn't scale. With daily data across many pages, products, or
regions, you need an automated framework that:

- Builds a dynamic baseline per series instead of one global threshold
- Flags spikes that are statistically unusual, not just big in absolute terms
- Handles both flavors of spike: the one-day blip and the multi-day slow burn
- Ranks what it finds, because an alert list nobody can triage is the same as no alert list

Wikipedia pageviews are a perfect testbed. The data is real, it's public, and the summer
of 2024 (Olympics, an assassination attempt, a presidential race) is packed with genuine
media-driven spikes to find.

## Step 2: The partitioned Parquet warehouse

The engineering payoff is in the storage layer. The fetch script writes Hive-style
month-partitioned Parquet (`year_month=YYYY-MM/`), the same pattern the
[Data Foundation](../data-foundation/data-engineering.html) page describes, just at a
coarser grain than production hourly data would use.

::: {.callout-note}
**On partition grain.** I deliberately partition by month, not by day, even though the
data is daily. With 103 articles × 92 days, a per-day partition would mean ~100 rows per
file, small enough that Parquet's per-file metadata overhead dominates the actual column
data and the format loses to plain CSV. Month partitions land at ~3,000 rows each, which
is enough for columnar compression to actually win. The same dataset at hourly bulk-dump
grain (~7M rows/hour) would partition by date with ease. Pick the partition column to
match the grain of the data, not the calendar.

## Step 3: Plant the answer key

Here's the part most anomaly detection writeups skip. If I only run the detector on raw
data and point at dramatic charts, you have to take my word that the flags are good.
Real spikes don't arrive labeled. So before detection, I plant **four fake spikes with
exact known dates, sizes, and durations** on top of the real data. That gives the
validation step an answer key: we know precisely what a perfect detector should find,
because we put it there.

The four are chosen to stress different failure modes, not picked at random:

| Article | Planted scenario | Multiplier | Duration | What it tests |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Albert_Einstein | "Breaking News" | ×4 | 2 days starting Jul 21 | a sharp burst on a steady page |
| Taylor_Swift | "Celebrity Mention" | ×3 | 3 days starting Aug 5 | a spike on a page that is already busy and noisy |
| Bitcoin | "Viral Social" | ×2 | 4 days starting Aug 25 | the smallest lift but the longest, a slow burn |
| Elon_Musk | "TV Mention" | ×5 | 1 day on Sep 9 | a one-day blip, the case a persistence rule misses |

The scenario names are just labels I made up for the shape of each event, they aren't
real news stories. ×N means every real pageview count inside that window gets multiplied
by N, so the spike sits on top of the page's genuine day-to-day texture instead of being
a clean synthetic curve.

The sizes are deliberately modest. In my experience real media-driven lifts land
somewhere between 2× and 5× of normal activity, a true 8× or 10× day is rare. An earlier
draft of this study planted ×8 and ×12 spikes and they were almost insulting to the
detector, anything would catch those. The ×2 Bitcoin slow burn is the one that actually
makes the detector work for it.

## Step 4: The detection algorithm

Robust statistics matter here. **Mean and standard deviation break down when outliers are
the thing you're trying to detect.** The outlier inflates the standard deviation, which
raises the threshold, which masks the next outlier. A classic feedback loop (there's a
chart of this failure further down).

The fix is **median + MAD (median absolute deviation)**. The median ignores extreme
values, and MAD scaled by 1.4826 is a consistent estimator of the standard deviation
under normality, so the z-scores stay interpretable.

Detection runs in two tiers, because real spikes come in two shapes:

- **Persistent tier:** robust z > 5 for at least 2 consecutive days. Catches sustained
  events while filtering one-day noise. This is the tier that earns the ×2 Bitcoin
  slow burn.
- **Extreme tier:** robust z > 12 on any single day. Exists because a genuinely large
  one-day event (our planted ×5 Elon Musk day lands around z ≈ 47) should not be thrown
  away just because it didn't persist.

```{python}
#| label: detection-algorithm
#| code-summary: "Robust tiered spike detection: rolling median + MAD"

### tuned on this dataset: z>5 alone flagged too much (daily wiki traffic is
### heavy tailed), z>5 with 2-day persistence missed the 1-day planted spike.
### two tiers handle both shapes.
WINDOW_DAYS = 21      ## 3 weeks of trailing history for the baseline
Z_PERSISTENT = 5.0    ## threshold for the persistent tier
Z_EXTREME = 12.0      ## a single day this far out counts on its own
MIN_PERSIST = 2       ## consecutive days required in the persistent tier

def detect_spikes_for_article(grp):
    """robust two-tier spike detection for one article's daily series"""
    grp = grp.sort_values('timestamp').reset_index(drop=True)

    ## rolling robust baseline
    grp['rolling_median'] = grp['views'].rolling(WINDOW_DAYS, min_periods=7).median()
    grp['rolling_mad'] = (
        grp['views'].rolling(WINDOW_DAYS, min_periods=7)
        .apply(lambda x: np.median(np.abs(x - np.median(x))), raw=True)
    )
    ### scale MAD so it's comparable to a std dev under normality
    grp['rolling_sigma'] = grp['rolling_mad'] * 1.4826

    ## z-score against the robust baseline
    grp['robust_z'] = (
        (grp['views'] - grp['rolling_median'])
        / grp['rolling_sigma'].replace(0, np.nan)
    )

    ## tier 1: persistent. find runs of consecutive days above Z_PERSISTENT
    ## and keep whole runs that last at least MIN_PERSIST days
    grp['above'] = grp['robust_z'] > Z_PERSISTENT
    run_id = (~grp['above']).cumsum()
    run_len = grp.groupby(run_id)['above'].transform('sum')
    grp['persistent'] = grp['above'] & (run_len >= MIN_PERSIST)

    ## tier 2: extreme. one day this far above baseline stands on its own
    grp['extreme'] = grp['robust_z'] > Z_EXTREME

    grp['is_spike'] = grp['persistent'] | grp['extreme']
    return grp

## manual loop instead of groupby().apply(), reads cleaner and dodges the
## include_groups deprecation dance in pandas 2.2+
per_article = []
for article_name, grp in df.groupby('article'):
    res = detect_spikes_for_article(grp.copy())
    res['article'] = article_name
    per_article.append(res)
results = pd.concat(per_article, ignore_index=True)

## group consecutive flagged days into distinct events, then rank.
## an alert list is only useful if it's ranked
flagged = results[results['is_spike']].copy()
flagged['gap'] = flagged.groupby('article')['timestamp'].diff().dt.days.fillna(99)
flagged['event_id'] = (flagged['gap'] > 1).groupby(flagged['article']).cumsum()

events = (
    flagged.groupby(['article', 'event_id'])
    .agg(
        start=('timestamp', 'min'),
        n_days=('is_spike', 'sum'),
        peak_z=('robust_z', 'max'),
        peak_views=('views', 'max'),
        baseline=('rolling_median', 'median'),
    )
    .reset_index()
    .sort_values('peak_z', ascending=False)
    .reset_index(drop=True)
)
events['lift'] = (events['peak_views'] / events['baseline']).round(1)

## peak date per event, the charts use this
peak_dates = []
for _, r in events.iterrows():
    sub = flagged[(flagged['article'] == r['article']) & (flagged['event_id'] == r['event_id'])]
    peak_dates.append(sub.loc[sub['robust_z'].idxmax(), 'timestamp'])
events['peak_date'] = peak_dates

n_events = len(events)
n_flagged_articles = events['article'].nunique()
print(f"{n_events} distinct events across {n_flagged_articles} of {n_articles} articles")
print(f"\ntop 12 events by peak robust z:")
show = events.head(12).copy()
show['start'] = show['start'].dt.date
show['peak_z'] = show['peak_z'].round(1)
print(show[['article', 'start', 'n_days', 'peak_z', 'lift']].to_string(index=False))
```

One honest note on precision: I don't report a false positive rate against the planted

## Why median + MAD instead of mean + std

This is the methodological core of the piece, so here's the failure made visible. Same
data (Taylor Swift with the planted ×3 spike), two detectors. The classic mean + 3·std
band swallows its own spike: the outlier days inflate the rolling std so much that the
threshold balloons above the data for the next three weeks. The robust band barely moves.

```{python}
#| label: robust-vs-classic
#| code-summary: "Classic mean+std threshold vs robust median+MAD on the same spike"
#| fig-cap: "Taylor Swift daily views with the planted ×3 spike. The classic threshold (dashed) inflates after contact with a spike and stays blind for a full window length, you can see it happen again after her real September spike. The robust threshold (solid navy) is barely disturbed."

ts = results[results['article'] == 'Taylor_Swift'].sort_values('timestamp').copy()

## classic detector on the identical window settings
ts['c_mean'] = ts['views'].rolling(WINDOW_DAYS, min_periods=7).mean()
ts['c_std'] = ts['views'].rolling(WINDOW_DAYS, min_periods=7).std()
ts['classic_upper'] = ts['c_mean'] + 3 * ts['c_std']
ts['robust_upper'] = ts['rolling_median'] + 3 * ts['rolling_sigma']

evt = [e for e in spike_events if e['article'] == 'Taylor_Swift'][0]
s = pd.Timestamp(evt['start'])
```

## What this demonstrates

**The methodology:** spike detection with median + MAD instead of mean + std, because the
anomalies you're hunting will corrupt classical statistics (the comparison chart above
shows exactly how). Two detection tiers because one-day blips and slow burns are both
real and one rule can't serve both.

**The validation:** I planted four spikes with known sizes and dates and graded the
detector against them, instead of pointing at charts and declaring victory. The grading
is day-level, not "did the article show up somewhere in the flag list."

**The honesty:** the thresholds were tuned on this dataset and I say so. The planted
events measure recall, not precision, and I say that too. The real events the detector
surfaced (Olympics, July 13, the DNC) are the strongest evidence it works, precisely
because I didn't plant them.

## What I'd extend in a production version

- **CUSUM or Bayesian online changepoint detection** for slow level shifts, the MAD
  approach is built for sharp spikes
- **Per-series threshold learning** — one global z threshold across 103 very different
  pages is the biggest simplification here; volatility profiles differ by page
- **Cause attribution** — join detected events against news APIs, social trends, or a
  media schedule so events arrive pre-explained instead of pre-ranked
- **Streaming implementation** — rolling median and MAD both have incremental versions,
  so this ports to a Kafka consumer for sub-minute detection at the hourly bulk-dump grain
