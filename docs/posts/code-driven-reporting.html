The Case for Code-Driven Reporting

Why your analytics team should stop building decks

Methodology
Engineering
Analytics Leadership
A methodology paper on treating reports as software products: version-controlled, reproducible, tested. Why most analytics teams drown in deliverables — and how to fix it.
Author

Jesus M

Published

May 20, 2026

Methodology Paper · 12 min read

TL;DR — Most analytics teams drown in manually assembled deliverables: PowerPoints, Excel files, ad-hoc dashboards. This is technical debt disguised as productivity. The fix is to treat reports as software products — version-controlled, parameterized, reproducible, and tested. This essay argues the case, lays out the maturity model, and walks through what it actually looks like in practice.

The problem nobody quite admits

Every analytics team I’ve worked, across financial services, pharma measurement, and ad-tech — has the a similar weekly routine. An analyst is hunched over a PowerPoint, copy-pasting numbers from Excel into a deck because the executive review is at 2 PM and the data refreshed at 11. Someone else is in Slack asking “wait, which version of the Q3 file is the real one?” A senior analyst is fixing a formula in a workbook that breaks every quarter when the date format shifts.

This is normal. It’s also a slow-motion disaster.

I’m going to make a strong claim: manually assembled deliverables are the single biggest source of avoidable risk and waste in modern analytics teams. Bigger than bad SQL. Bigger than tooling debates. Bigger than headcount. And yet most teams treat the problem as a soft cultural issue (“we should automate more”) rather than what it actually is: a structural choice with measurable cost.

This essay lays out the case for code-driven reporting — treating analytical deliverables the way software engineers treat applications. It’s not a tools post. It’s an argument about what analytics work is, and what it should be.

What “code-driven reporting” actually means

The term is broader than it sounds. I don’t just mean “use Python instead of Excel.” I mean a specific commitment:

Every analytical deliverable, every chart, table, metric, and narrative - should be reproducible from raw data with a single command.

The single-command test for analytics maturity.

If your team cannot explain where a number came from, what logic was applied, and how to reproduce it through a defined script or workflow, then the process is not mature analytics. It is handcrafted reporting. That has a place (research, exploration) but it shouldn’t be the output medium for recurring deliverables.

Code-driven reporting means:

  • Inputs are explicit. Source data is specified, dated, and pulled from a system of record, not “the file Sarah sent.”
  • Logic is in code. Filtering, aggregation, business rules, and metric definitions live in version controlled files, not in someone’s head or a hidden Excel formula.
  • Outputs are generated. The PDF, HTML report, Excel workbook, or slide deck is produced by a script that anyone on the team can run.
  • Changes are tracked. When the CFO asks “why did this number change from last quarter?” the answer is a git diff, not a memory test.
  • Reviews happen on the code. Pull requests force methodology discussions before reports go out.

That’s the full commitment. Anything less is a halfway state that incurs most of the cost of manual work without much of the benefit of automation.

The maturity model

Most teams sit somewhere on this spectrum. Be honest about where yours actually is.

Level 0: Manual/Artisanal reporting

Reports are assembled by hand each cycle. Numbers come from running queries, copying outputs, screenshots, and pasting into PowerPoint or Excel. Senior analysts become bottlenecks because they are often the only ones who know how each metric is calculated.

Cost signal: When someone leaves the team, reporting capability degrades for weeks.

Level 1: Templated reporting

A spreadsheet or PowerPoint template exists. Data still gets pasted in manually, but at least the formatting is consistent. Charts may update from source ranges, but the process still depends on someone moving the data into place.

Cost signal: Quarter end still takes three or more days of analyst time, every time.

Level 2: Scripted extraction

Scripts pull data from source systems and produce intermediate outputs like CSVs, summary tables, or refreshed datasets. This is progress, but a human still has to assemble, validate, and explain the final deliverable.

Cost signal: Reports go out, but the team still has to manually investigate what changed and why.

Level 3: Parameterized reporting

A Quarto, R Markdown, Python, Jupyter notebook, or similar workflow can render the full deliverable from raw data. Parameters control the client, date range, market, channel, or scope. One clear process produces the final artifact.

Cost signal: New report versions take hours, not days.

Level 4: Continuous reporting

Reports run automatically on a schedule or when the data refreshes. Outputs are versioned, archived, and easy to access. Methodology changes go through review. Basic checks catch broken logic, missing data, or unexpected changes before stakeholders see the output.

Cost signal: The team spends most of its time answering new analytical questions, not recreating the same reports over and over.

Most teams I have seen, including good ones, sit at Level 1 or Level 2 and think they are at Level 3.

The honest test is simple: pick last quarter’s executive deck. Can someone who was not involved in the original work reproduce it from the raw data this morning and explain where the numbers came from before lunch?

If the answer is no, the team is below Level 3.

Why teams stay stuck

The objections are predictable. They’re also mostly wrong.

“We don’t have time to build infrastructure.” This is the most common objection and it’s exactly backwards. The time you don’t have is being consumed by the manual work itself. The investment pays back in weeks for any recurring report. The math is brutal: a 40-hour build that saves 4 hours/week breaks even in 10 weeks and saves 200+ hours/year thereafter. I have never seen a team where the math didn’t work.

“Our stakeholders want PowerPoint.” They want the information in a format they know how to read. They do not care how the PowerPoint was made.

A scripted workflow can still produce a PowerPoint file. The difference is that the deck can have consistent formatting, accurate numbers, and a clear trail back to the source data and logic.

The stakeholder gets the format they want. The analytics team gets a process that does not depend on manual rebuilding every cycle.

“Our data is too messy.” Yes. That is exactly why the cleaning logic needs to be documented and stored in code.

Messy data handled manually is where mistakes spread quietly. Messy data handled through repeatable logic is where upstream issues get caught earlier.

The point is not that the data has to be perfect. The point is that the logic should not live only in someone’s memory.

“We’re not engineers.” You do not need to be a software engineer to make reporting reproducible.

Tools like Quarto, R Markdown, Jupyter, dbt, Python, R, and SQL are built for this kind of work. Analysts do not need to turn into engineers, but they do need to take the process seriously.

If a report goes out every week, every month, or every quarter, the workflow should be treated like something the business depends on, because it does.

“It’s not worth it for one-off analyses.” Agreed.

Not every analysis needs a full process around it. Exploration can be messy. Research can be flexible. Not every question deserves a pipeline.

The discipline applies for reacurrent deliverables.

If the same report, metric, deck, or analysis keeps coming back, then it should not keep being rebuilt from scratch.

What it costs to stay at Level 1

I want to put real numbers around this because the cost of manual reporting is easy to ignore until someone measures it.

A team I worked with had five analysts and one director. Each month, the team produced 12 recurring client reports plus ad hoc work. Manual assembly took about 6 hours per report, plus another 2 hours of senior review.

That is 96 hours per month spent on production work alone.Across the team, that was roughly 30% of total analyst capacity going to producing things that had been produced 50+ times before

For the people most involved in the reporting cycle, that represented a meaningful share of available reporting capacity. Time that could have gone toward actual analysis was being spent rebuilding deliverables that had already been produced dozens of times before.

After moving the recurring reports to a code driven pipeline using Quarto, parameterized R, and a scheduler, production time dropped to about 4 hours per month for the same 12 reports.

The reclaimed time went back into actual analytical work: improving methodology, building better measurement frameworks, investigating performance changes, and having deeper client conversations.

The build took two analysts about six weeks of partial effort.

It saved roughly 1,100 analyst hours per year.

That is more than half of a full time analyst recovered from one project, with the savings continuing every year after.

This is not some rare outcome. For recurring reporting, this is usually what happens when the process is rebuilt the right way.

What “doing it right” looks like in practice

Here’s the structure I converge on, regardless of language:

project/
├── data/                # Raw data (gitignored if large)
├── Python/R/ or src/    # Cleaning, transformation, modeling functions
├── reports/             # Quarto/RMarkdown/Jupyter sources
│   ├── _quarto.yml      # Site/book config
│   ├── monthly_review.qmd
│   └── client_X_report.qmd
├── output/              # Rendered deliverables (gitignored)
├── tests/               # Tests for transformation functions
├── .venv/renv.lock or pyproject.toml   # Pinned dependencies
└── README.md            # How to run it

The non-negotiables:

  1. Pinned dependencies. renv for R, uv/pyproject.toml for Python. The pipeline must run identically on a teammate’s laptop a year from now.
  2. Functions live separately from reports. The .qmd calls functions; it doesn’t define them. This makes everything testable.
  3. Tests on transformations, not reports. Test that compute_cpa() returns the right number for known inputs. Don’t test the rendered HTML.
  4. Parameterization is first-class. Reports take parameters (date range, client ID). Variants are configuration, not copies.
  5. Outputs are reproducible from main. The current branch should always produce a runnable pipeline.

The leadership angle

This isn’t a tooling argument. It’s a leadership argument.

Analytics managers and directors who insist on code-driven reporting do three things at once:

They protect their team from burnout. Manual deliverable production is the work analysts complain about most. It’s repetitive, error-prone, and high-stakes. Eliminating it makes the job better.

They protect the business from quiet errors.

A mistake in a manually built deck can sit there for weeks before anyone notices. A copied number, a stale chart, or a missed filter can make its way into a client meeting or executive review.

A repeatable workflow does not remove every risk, but it gives the team a better chance to catch issues earlier. Logic can be reviewed. Outputs can be checked. Changes can be traced back to the source.

They build a team that scales. A team at Level 3 or higher can take on more work without needing headcount to grow at the same rate. A team stuck at Level 1 needs more analyst time every time the reporting volume increases.

That is not a sustainable operating model. It turns talented analysts into report producers instead of problem solvers.

The best analytics leaders I know do not measure their teams by the number of decks produced. They measure by the number of recurring deliverables that no longer require manual assembly.

A useful KPI for analytics leadership.

If you are an analytics leader and your team is at Level 1 or Level 2, you have a budget problem. You are paying for it in analyst time.

If you are an analytics individual contributor and your team is at Level 1 or Level 2, you have a career problem. Your most valuable hours are being spent on work that does not build your skills, improve the business, or compound over time.

Where to start

If you’re convinced and want to move, here’s the sequence that has worked for me:

  1. Pick one recurring report. Not the most complex. Pick one that runs at least monthly, has a stable structure, and would be embarrassing if it broke.
  2. Reproduce it in Quarto or RMarkdown. Match the existing format exactly. The goal is not to improve the report — it’s to demonstrate that the code can produce the same output.
  3. Run both side-by-side for one cycle. Generate the manual version and the automated version. Check that they match. Fix the discrepancies.
  4. Switch over. The next cycle, only run the automated version. Keep the manual process as fallback for one more cycle, then retire it.
  5. Repeat. Move the next report. Then the next. Within a year you’ll have inverted the team’s time distribution.

The hardest part is the first one. The seventh report takes 1/4 the time of the first. The team builds shared infrastructure as they go.

Closing

Code-driven reporting is not a fashion choice or a technical preference. It’s a strategic decision about how an analytics team allocates its scarcest resource — analyst time — and how it manages its largest hidden risk: silent errors in artisanal work.

The teams that take this seriously will spend their time on harder, more valuable problems. The teams that don’t will keep producing decks until automation does it for them. There is no third option.
